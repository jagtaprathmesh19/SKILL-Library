# Psychology & Mental Health at Work Reference

## The 3 Misdiagnosed Career Conditions

These are consistently confused with each other. The intervention is completely different for each.

### 1. Burnout
**Definition:** Chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed. (WHO, ICD-11)

**Three dimensions (Maslach Burnout Inventory):**
- **Exhaustion**: Feeling depleted at a level rest doesn't fix
- **Cynicism / Depersonalization**: Emotional distance from the work, colleagues, or mission
- **Reduced efficacy**: Feeling like nothing you do matters or is good enough

**Key signal:** The person used to care. Burnout requires caring first, then losing it.

**What helps:** Recovery (actual rest), workload reduction, boundaries, addressing the systemic cause (often overwork + lack of control + lack of recognition)

**What doesn't help:** Pushing through, more productivity systems, "positive thinking"

---

### 2. Boredom / Stagnation
**Definition:** The work no longer challenges them. They're capable of much more than they're doing.

**Key signal:** They're not exhausted — they're understimulated. They check out rather than burn out.

**What helps:** New projects, stretch assignments, a promotion push, a lateral move to a more complex role, side projects

**What doesn't help:** Rest (they're not tired). Therapy (they're not unwell). They need *challenge*.

---

### 3. Wrong Fit
**Definition:** The role, industry, or values of the workplace are misaligned with who they are.

**Sub-types:**
- Right industry, wrong role (e.g., a great analyst who hates analysis)
- Right role, wrong industry (e.g., a great marketer who hates what they're marketing)
- Right everything, wrong company culture (e.g., collaborative person in a cutthroat environment)

**Key signal:** They feel this way across multiple companies, not just the current one.

**What helps:** Career exploration (see main SKILL.md), repositioning, and in some cases, a career change

**Misdiagnosis trap:** Treating wrong-fit as burnout leads to "rest" that doesn't help, because the problem persists when they return.

## Imposter Syndrome

**What it is:** Persistent belief that you are not as competent as others perceive you to be, and that you will be "found out."

**Who gets it:** Predominantly high achievers. Studies show it's prevalent in 70% of people at some point. It is more common in environments where the person is a minority, first-generation professional, or high-visibility.

**Why it's irrational (and how to show this):**
Incompetent people generally don't worry about being incompetent. Worrying about being "found out" is often itself a sign of competence and self-awareness.

### Intervention Framework

**Step 1: Evidence Audit**
Ask them to list:
- 5 things they've accomplished that they're proud of
- 3 times someone praised them specifically (not generically)
- 2 hard problems they solved that others couldn't

This externalizes the evidence their brain is filtering out.

**Step 2: Externalize the Critic**
"If a close friend described your career to a stranger, what would they say?" Usually this reveals the gap between how others see them and how they see themselves.

**Step 3: Reframe "Not Knowing" as Contextual**
Nobody knows everything. Competence isn't omniscience — it's knowing what you know, knowing what you don't, and knowing how to figure out the rest.

**Step 4: Name the Pattern**
Sometimes just naming imposter syndrome ("What you're describing has a name") is itself relieving. It de-pathologizes the experience.

## Fear of Career Change

People often "know" what they want but freeze. This is almost always fear, not confusion.

**Common fears to name and address:**

| Fear | What to Say |
|---|---|
| "I'll fail and have to come back" | "Most pivots aren't irreversible. What would you do if it didn't work out? Could you live with that?" |
| "I'll lose my salary/status" | "That's often temporary. Map out the financial timeline: how long until you're back to current income in the new field?" |
| "I'm too old / too late" | "Statistically, most people underestimate how many years of career they have left. What's the age at which you'd stop worrying about this?" |
| "I don't have the credentials" | "What does the actual job posting require vs. what you have? The gap is often smaller than it feels." |
| "People will think I'm a failure" | "Who specifically? And what do you think they'd actually say?" (often surfaces that the fear is projection) |

## Communicating About Mental Health at Work

When users are dealing with anxiety, depression, or other mental health conditions at work:

**Their legal rights (US):**
- ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act): Mental health conditions can qualify for reasonable accommodation
- FMLA: Up to 12 weeks unpaid leave for serious health conditions (including mental health)
- Accommodation requests can include: modified schedule, remote work, quiet workspace, reduced responsibilities temporarily

**What to share:**
- They are not required to disclose a diagnosis to an employer
- They *can* request accommodation without disclosing specifics
- A doctor's note confirming need for accommodation is usually sufficient

**When to suggest professional support:**
Use a bridge, not a prescription. Example:
> "What you're describing sounds like it's affecting you beyond the job itself. Have you considered talking to someone — a therapist or counselor — alongside thinking through the career piece? The two often help each other."

## Navigating a Layoff (Emotional Arc)

Layoffs trigger grief. The emotional stages are real and don't skip.

**Typical arc:**
1. **Shock / Disbelief** (day 1–3): Don't make any major decisions here. Rest.
2. **Anger / Resentment** (day 3–14): Valid. Don't send that email. Write it, don't send it.
3. **Anxiety about the future** (week 2–4): This is when to start building a plan.
4. **Reorientation** (month 1–2): Identity shifts from "employee at X" to "professional looking for the right next thing"
5. **Momentum** (month 2+): Search becomes a job. Systems and routines matter.

**What to say at each stage:**
- Shock: "That's a lot to process. You don't have to have a plan today."
- Anger: "That reaction makes complete sense. What would feel helpful right now?"
- Anxiety: "Let's build a structure. Breaking this into concrete steps usually reduces the overwhelm."
- Reorientation: "What do you want the next chapter to look like? This is actually an opportunity to be intentional."

**Practical first steps after layoff:**
1. File for unemployment benefits immediately (even if unsure you qualify — most do)
2. Review severance agreement carefully before signing — you often have 21 days
3. Negotiate severance: additional weeks, continued health coverage, LinkedIn recommendation, reference letter
4. Update LinkedIn *before* updating resume — recruiters will find you
5. Tell your network immediately — most jobs come through people, not postings
6. Give yourself 1–2 weeks before going full-speed into search (if financially possible)
