# Salary Negotiation Reference

## The Fundamental Truth

Negotiating is expected. Recruiters and hiring managers know the first offer is not always the final offer. 85% of employers have flexibility. Only 15% of candidates negotiate. Being in that 15% is one of the highest-ROI career moves you can make.

Even a $5,000 increase at $80K base compounds to $100,000+ over 10 years when factoring in raises, bonuses, and future job offers anchored to your current salary.

---

## The 5 Rules

1. **Never give a number first** — Whoever anchors first loses leverage. Always ask for their range.
2. **Research before you negotiate** — Know your market value from at least 3 sources.
3. **Negotiate the full package** — Base salary is just one lever. Use all of them.
4. **Stay warm, not weak** — Firmness and enthusiasm aren't opposites. Sound excited AND clear.
5. **Get it in writing** — Don't stop negotiating until you have a written offer with all terms confirmed.

---

## Market Research Sources

| Source | Best For |
|---|---|
| Levels.fyi | Tech roles (especially engineering, PM, data) — equity + total comp |
| Glassdoor | General salary ranges, often slightly understated |
| LinkedIn Salary | Role + location combinations, based on self-reported data |
| Payscale | Non-tech roles, detailed by years of experience |
| Built In | Startup / tech startup salaries with equity breakdown |
| Bureau of Labor Statistics | Official government occupational wage data |
| Industry surveys | Trade associations often publish annual compensation reports |
| Informational interviews | The most accurate — ask peers in similar roles |

**Strategy:** Collect 3+ data points, identify the 60th–75th percentile for your experience level, and anchor your ask there. Don't anchor at the median — you'll negotiate down from your ask.

## Full Negotiation Package Checklist

Most people only negotiate base. Negotiate all of these:

- [ ] **Base salary** — The most important lever; affects all percentage-based items
- [ ] **Signing bonus** — Especially powerful if leaving unvested equity or mid-year
- [ ] **Annual bonus** — Target percentage, guaranteed first-year minimum
- [ ] **Equity** — Grant size, vesting schedule (4yr/1yr cliff standard), refresh grants
- [ ] **Remote policy** — Fully remote, hybrid days, flexibility to work from another city
- [ ] **PTO / vacation** — Additional days, rollover, mental health days
- [ ] **Title** — Matters for future job offers; "Senior" vs. "Lead" can mean $20K difference next job
- [ ] **Start date** — Can buy you time to finish current project, take a break, or capture unvested comp
- [ ] **Professional development** — Annual budget for courses, conferences, certifications
- [ ] **Equipment** — Laptop spec, home office stipend
- [ ] **Health insurance** — Premium coverage tier; dental/vision quality

## Scripts: Every Scenario

### When Asked for Salary Expectations (Before Offer)

**Deflect — Version 1 (Soft):**
> "I want to make sure I understand the full scope of the role before anchoring on a number. Could you share what you've budgeted for this position?"

**Deflect — Version 2 (If they push):**
> "I'm flexible and focused on finding the right fit. If you can share the range, I can confirm whether we're aligned and we can keep moving forward."

**Deflect — Version 3 (If they push hard):**
> "Based on my research, roles like this typically range from [X] to [Y] in this market. I'd expect to be somewhere in that range, but I'm more focused on the opportunity itself. What does your budget look like?"

---

### When You Receive an Offer

**Never accept or reject on the spot.** Always say:
> "Thank you so much — I'm genuinely excited. I'd like to take a day to review everything carefully and come back to you. Is [specific day/time] OK?"

This is not rude. It is expected. A company that pressures you to decide immediately is a yellow flag.

---

### Countering the Offer

**Rule:** Counter exactly once, clearly, with a specific number — not a range.

**Standard counter:**
> "I've done some research and given the [specific experience / scope / market], I was hoping to land at [X]. Is there flexibility to get there?"

**If you have a competing offer:**
> "I want to be fully transparent — I have another offer at [Y]. But this role is my first choice because [genuine specific reason]. If you can get to [X], I'm ready to commit today."

**Counter with rationale (stronger):**
> "I've benchmarked this against [Levels.fyi / Glassdoor / peers in similar roles], and for someone with [specific experience], the market range is [X–Y]. I'd love to land at [X] — is that achievable?"

---

### Negotiating Around "The Range Is Fixed"

This is almost never entirely true. Pivot to other levers:

> "I understand the base is set. Could we explore a signing bonus to bridge the gap? Or additional equity? I want to make this work — what flexibility do we have in the rest of the package?"

---

### When They Come Back Below Your Ask

**If close:** Accept or ask for one more thing:
> "I appreciate you going back to the team. I can make [their number] work if we can include [one more item — signing bonus, extra PTO, equity bump]. Does that work?"

**If far from your ask:**
> "I really appreciate the effort. I have to be honest — there's still a meaningful gap. Is there any way to get to [X], even with a first-year performance review for an adjustment?"

---

### Negotiating a Raise (Internal)

**Step 1:** Build your case before the conversation, not in it. Prepare:
- A list of accomplishments (quantified) since your last review
- Market data showing you're underpaid relative to peers
- A specific number (anchor at 15–20% above your target)

**Step 2:** Request a dedicated meeting (not in passing):
> "I'd love to schedule some time to talk about my compensation. I've been building out my case and want to have a real conversation about it."

**Step 3:** The conversation:
> "Over the past [period], I've [3 key accomplishments with numbers]. I've also benchmarked my role in this market and found that similar roles are compensating at [X]. I'd like to discuss getting my salary to [Y]."

**If denied:**
> "I understand the budget constraints. Can we agree on specific milestones that, if hit, would justify this adjustment at the next review? I'd like to be working toward something concrete."

## Equity 101 (for users confused about stock comp)

**Types:**
- **RSUs (Restricted Stock Units)**: Company gives you shares that vest over time. Value = # shares × current stock price.
- **Stock Options (ISOs/NSOs)**: Right to *buy* shares at a fixed price (strike price). Valuable only if stock price exceeds strike.
- **Phantom Equity / Profit Interest**: Common at private companies; paper ownership, not actual shares.

**Key terms to always ask:**
- What is the current valuation / last round price?
- What is the total shares outstanding? (to understand your % ownership)
- What is the vesting schedule? (4 years, 1-year cliff is standard)
- What is the strike price? (for options)
- Is there a change-of-control provision? (do options accelerate in acquisition?)

**Red flag:** A company that won't share its valuation or cap table structure before you join is hiding something.
