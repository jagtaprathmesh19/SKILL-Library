# Interview Preparation Reference

## The Meta-Framework

Every interview has one goal for the interviewer: answer the question "Can this person do the job, and will working with them be good?" Your job is to make both answers obviously "yes."

Three things that win interviews:
1. **Clarity** — You communicate your experience precisely and concisely
2. **Evidence** — You back every claim with a specific story or number
3. **Interest** — You show you've done the homework and actually want *this* role, not just any role

---

## "Tell Me About Yourself" (The Most Important 90 Seconds)

**The Past → Present → Future → Why Here formula:**

> "I started my career in [X], where I [quick highlight]. Over the past [N] years, I moved into [Y] and most recently [key accomplishment or role]. Right now, I'm looking to [future direction] — and [Company] caught my attention because [specific, researched reason]. I think what I bring to this role specifically is [1–2 differentiators]."

Practice this until it sounds natural, not rehearsed. It should be 60–90 seconds.

## The STAR Method (for all behavioral questions)

- **S**ituation: Brief context (1–2 sentences max)
- **T**ask: What were you responsible for?
- **A**ction: What specifically did *you* do? (Not "we" — interviewers want to know your contribution)
- **R**esult: What happened? Always quantify if possible.

**Upgraded version — STARL:**
Add **L**earning: "What I took from that experience was..."
This shows self-awareness and growth mindset — especially important for failure/challenge questions.

## The 8 Core Story Bank

Help the user identify one strong story for each theme before any behavioral interview:

| Theme | Common Question Trigger |
|---|---|
| **Leadership** | "Tell me about a time you led a team or project" |
| **Failure** | "Tell me about a time you failed or made a mistake" |
| **Conflict** | "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a colleague or manager" |
| **Initiative** | "Tell me about a time you went above and beyond" |
| **Teamwork** | "Tell me about a time you worked cross-functionally" |
| **Data/Analysis** | "Tell me about a time you made a data-driven decision" |
| **Ambiguity** | "Tell me about a time you worked without clear direction" |
| **Achievement** | "What's your biggest professional accomplishment?" |

One strong, specific story per theme. A great candidate repurposes 3–4 stories across multiple questions by emphasizing different angles.

## Question-by-Question Playbook

### "Why do you want to work here?"

**Wrong answer:** "I love your mission and culture."
**Right answer:** Demonstrate research. Name:
- A specific product, decision, or achievement of theirs you respect
- Why *this* role at *this* stage of their growth makes sense for you
- A specific challenge or goal of theirs that you're excited to contribute to

> "I've been following [Company]'s expansion into [market] for about a year. The way you approached [specific strategy] was genuinely smart, and I think I could contribute meaningfully to [specific problem area]. That, plus talking to [Name] who works on your team — it confirmed this is the environment I want."

### "What's your greatest weakness?"

**The formula:** Real weakness + what you've done to address it + current status

**Wrong:** "I work too hard" / "I'm a perfectionist"
**Right:** A genuine, role-adjacent weakness that you've made progress on.

> "Early in my career, I struggled to delegate — I'd hold onto tasks because I thought it was faster to do them myself. I've been intentional about this: I now do a weekly check-in with my team specifically to offload appropriate work. It's improved both my output and their growth."

### "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"

Align with what the company can realistically offer. If it's a small company with limited leadership growth, don't say "I want to be a VP." If it's a large company, show ambition but within their structure.

> "Honestly, I want to get really good at [core skill of this role] first. In 5 years, I'd love to be the person people come to for [area of expertise]. Whether that's in a management or IC track, I'm open — I care more about depth and impact than title."

### "Why are you leaving your current role?"

**Never:** Badmouth your employer, manager, or colleagues.
**Frame as:** Moving toward, not running from.

> "I've learned a lot at [Company], and I'm proud of what we built. But I've grown as much as I can in this structure, and I'm ready for [a bigger scope / a different type of challenge / an environment that moves faster]. That's what drew me to this conversation."

### "What are your salary expectations?"

Delay and flip if possible:
> "I'd love to understand the full scope of the role before anchoring on a number. Could you share the budgeted range?"

If pressed:
> "Based on my research and the level of this role, I'm targeting something in the [X–Y] range — but I'm flexible depending on the full package."

## Closing the Interview

Never say "No, I think I've covered everything" when asked if you have questions. This is a golden opportunity to show intelligence and genuine interest.

**Strong closing questions:**

1. "What does success look like in this role after 90 days? After a year?"
2. "What's the biggest challenge the team is facing right now?"
3. "How would you describe the culture here — and how does it differ from what you expected when you joined?"
4. "What made you decide to stay at [Company]?" (For interviewers who've been there 2+ years)
5. "What's your timeline for making a decision, and what are the next steps?"

**Close with energy:**
> "I want you to know — this conversation has genuinely increased my interest. I'm excited about [specific aspect], and I'd love to continue the conversation."

## Mock Interview Protocol

When the user wants to practice:

1. Ask what type of interview: behavioral / technical / case / presentation / final round
2. Ask what role and company (so you can tailor questions)
3. Run 4–5 questions, one at a time
4. After each answer, give specific feedback:
   - What worked well (be specific)
   - What to cut or tighten
   - What was missing (evidence, numbers, resolution)
   - A suggested improvement to one part of the answer
5. At the end, give an overall assessment: ready / nearly ready / needs work on [specific area]

## Handling Curveball Questions

**"If you were a [animal/superhero/tool], what would you be?"**
They're testing personality and self-awareness, not the answer. Pick something and connect it to a real trait.
> "Probably a Swiss Army knife — I'm most energized when I can shift between strategic and hands-on work, and I tend to be the person people come to when they need someone versatile."

**"Sell me this pen."**
They want to see if you ask clarifying questions before pitching. Say: "Before I pitch it, can I ask — what do you use pens for most? What would make an ideal pen for you?" Then pitch to what they said.

**Illegal questions** (age, marital status, religion, nationality, pregnancy):
You don't have to answer. Options:
- Redirect: "I'm not sure how that relates to the role — happy to speak to my qualifications though."
- Answer if you're comfortable
- Note it as a culture signal and factor into your decision
