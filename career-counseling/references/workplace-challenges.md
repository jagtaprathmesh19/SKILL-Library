# Workplace Challenges Reference

## Triage Decision Tree

Before advising, always determine: **Fix or Exit?**

**Fix indicators:**
- Problem is isolated to one person or one project
- Company/culture is otherwise good
- Manager or situation is new and could change
- User has leverage (good performance, tenure, relationships)
- Problem is solvable with communication or structural change

**Exit indicators:**
- Problem is cultural or leadership-level (not one person)
- Pattern repeats across multiple roles/managers at same company
- User has raised concerns and been dismissed or retaliated against
- Physical or mental health is being significantly affected
- Trust is broken and irreparable

When in doubt: "Is this a problem you want to solve, or are you looking for permission to leave?"

## Toxic Workplace

**Signs (beyond just "it's hard"):**
- Psychological safety is absent — people are afraid to speak up, ask questions, or admit mistakes
- Blame culture: mistakes are punished rather than learned from
- Credit is routinely taken by managers or peers
- Boundaries around personal time are not respected (expected availability 24/7)
- DEI issues: marginalization, favoritism, discrimination, or hostility toward certain groups
- Leadership is inconsistent, unpredictable, or retaliatory

**Immediate steps:**
1. **Document everything** — dates, times, witnesses, exact words. Do this in personal notes/email, not company systems.
2. **Identify allies** — Is there a trusted HR contact, skip-level manager, or peer who can help?
3. **Know your rights** — Many toxic behaviors constitute hostile work environment under employment law
4. **Start a quiet exit plan** — Update resume and LinkedIn, reach out to network — before anything escalates

**Scripts for documenting:**
- After a verbal incident, send a follow-up email: "Per our conversation today, I wanted to document my understanding of what was discussed..." (creates paper trail without confrontation)
- Keep a personal log: date, what happened, who was present, how it affected you

**When to go to HR:**
HR protects the company, not the employee. That said, some violations require HR involvement:
- Harassment (sexual, racial, religious, disability-based)
- Retaliation for protected activity (whistleblowing, requesting accommodation, etc.)
- Safety violations

**Before going to HR:** Consult an employment attorney for a free initial consultation. Know what you're walking into.

## Difficult Manager

**Types and approaches:**

| Manager Type | Strategy |
|---|---|
| **Micromanager** | Over-communicate proactively; give updates before they ask; build trust with track record |
| **Credit stealer** | CC relevant stakeholders on your work; document contributions in writing; build lateral relationships |
| **Inconsistent / unpredictable** | Ask for written expectations; recap conversations via email; reduce ambiguity you control |
| **Passive-aggressive** | Name the behavior directly but neutrally: "I want to make sure I'm understanding your feedback clearly" |
| **Checked-out / doesn't support you** | Manage up to their manager; build your own visibility; find a sponsor elsewhere |

**The "Managing Up" Conversation:**
> "I want to make sure I'm giving you what you need. What does success look like for this project from your perspective? And how do you prefer to be updated along the way?"

**When to escalate to their manager:**
- You've tried addressing it directly and it hasn't changed
- The situation is affecting your ability to do your job
- There's documented evidence of the behavior
- Frame it as "seeking guidance" not "complaining": "I'd appreciate your perspective on how to handle X."

## Conflict with a Colleague

**The SBI Framework (Situation-Behavior-Impact):**

A non-accusatory structure for addressing conflict:

1. **Situation**: "In our meeting on Tuesday..."
2. **Behavior**: "...when you interrupted me mid-presentation..."
3. **Impact**: "...I felt undermined in front of the client, and I'm concerned it affected their confidence in the project."

Then: "I'd like to understand your perspective and figure out how we can work together better going forward."

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't CC their manager on the first conversation — this escalates immediately and damages trust
- Don't send a long emotional email — have the conversation in person or on video
- Don't loop in other colleagues to "take sides" — this poisons the team

**If direct conversation doesn't work:**
Request a mediated conversation with HR or a mutual manager. Frame it as "wanting to improve collaboration" not "I have a complaint."

## Being Passed Over for Promotion

**Validate first.** This is genuinely painful, especially if they worked hard for it.

**Then diagnose — which box does it fall into?**

1. **Performance gap**: They're not yet at the next level's expectations (be honest)
2. **Visibility gap**: Their work is good but leadership doesn't know it
3. **Sponsorship gap**: No one is advocating for them in the decision room
4. **Political/structural**: Budget freeze, favoritism, structural bias

**If performance gap:**
> "What specifically does your manager say about what 'next level' looks like? Are those expectations written down anywhere?"

Help them get explicit, documented criteria — and then build a plan against them.

**If visibility gap:**
- Weekly "impact updates" to manager (3 bullets: what I did, what resulted, what's next)
- Volunteer to present in broader meetings
- Find a cross-functional project with executive exposure

**If sponsorship gap:**
- Identify 1–2 senior leaders who know their work (not just their manager)
- Have a direct conversation: "I'm working toward [promotion]. I'd value your perspective on what I should focus on — and whether you'd be willing to speak to my work when the conversation comes up."

**The Promotion Conversation script:**
> "I've been in this role for [X time], and I'm committed to it. I'd love to have a direct conversation about what promotion to [next level] would look like, and what I need to demonstrate to get there. Can we set time for that?"

## Being Underpaid

**Research first (load salary-negotiation.md).**

If they're clearly underpaid, the conversation is a correction, not a request for a favor.

**The script:**
> "I've been doing some research on compensation for [role] at my level in this market, and I'm finding that the range is typically [X–Y]. I've been at [current salary] for [time period]. I'd like to discuss getting my compensation in line with the market."

**If denied:**
> "I understand. Can we agree on what would need to change — either in my responsibilities or timeline — for this to happen? I'd like to be working toward something concrete."

If denied with no path forward, that is information. Begin a quiet external search.

## The Quit-or-Stay Decision Framework

When users are genuinely torn, walk them through this:

**Score each dimension 1–5 (5 = great, 1 = terrible):**

| Dimension | Score |
|---|---|
| Day-to-day work (interesting, engaging?) | |
| Manager relationship | |
| Team / colleagues | |
| Compensation & benefits | |
| Growth trajectory | |
| Company mission / values alignment | |
| Work-life balance / sustainability | |
| Career impact of staying 2 more years | |

**Interpretation:**
- 32–40: Strong stay — unless 1–2 dimensions are critically broken
- 22–31: Mixed — identify which low scores are fixable vs. structural
- Below 22: The data is telling you something. What's keeping you?

**The "Regret Minimization" question** (from Jeff Bezos):
> "When you're 80 years old, which decision will you regret more — staying too long, or leaving too soon?"

This often cuts through the noise faster than any framework.
